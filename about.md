# About

I was born into an immigrant family and moved across several countries before settling in the United States. Through all of it I carried one question that never went away: why do humans age, and can it be slowed?

I started with computation, because data was the most accessible entry point. As a Bioinformatics & Product Associate at PGxAI, I worked across genetic data analysis, AI model development, database architecture, and SOC 2 / HIPAA compliance and co-authored five peer-reviewed and submitted papers in venues including Nature Portfolio, Mayo Clinic Proceedings, and JAMIA. That work taught me how clinical genomics actually becomes medical practice, not just how it looks on paper.

I then joined Dr. Michael Snyder's lab at Stanford University School of Medicine, working directly with Brady Hislop. I built a Python package integrating the KEGG, NCBI, and CAZy databases to map orthologous genes across human gut microbiome species, and an automated BLAST pipeline for cross-species genomic and proteomic pattern detection. Seeing multi-omics applied to human systems biology firsthand is what convinced me that the future of aging research is integrative and translational.

## Why MD-PhD

Aging is a complex, multi-scale problem. A PhD alone narrows you into a single specialty; the clinical side matters because translating aging interventions — trials, IND submissions, real interventions in real patients — requires clinical credibility alongside molecular depth. An MD-PhD gives me both, and the standing to do the work rather than have decisions made for me.

My specific interest is the intersection of pharmacogenomics and geroprotection: predicting, by genotype, which patients actually benefit from interventions such as rapamycin, metformin, and NAD+ precursors. Precision medicine for healthspan, not one-size-fits-all longevity claims.
